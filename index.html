Guide 01

Dr. Ijay's Self-Care Stack:
What I Actually Use

I spend my nights admitting patients for conditions that are often preventable. These are the 10 things I actually do — not just tell my patients to do — to protect my own health.

"I'm a hospitalist. I see what happens when we ignore our bodies long enough. These aren't trendy wellness tips — they're what actually moves the needle." — Dr. Ijay
01
Body

I protect my sleep like it's a clinical order

As a nocturnist, my sleep schedule is unconventional — but non-negotiable. Sleep deprivation increases cortisol, impairs glucose metabolism, and raises cardiovascular risk. I use blackout curtains, a sleep mask, phone on Do Not Disturb, and I treat my sleep window like a prescription I'm filling.

02
Nutrition

I eat protein at every single meal

Not for aesthetics — for blood sugar stability, satiety, and muscle preservation. I aim for 25–30g per meal. On night shifts, this means prepping high-protein snacks in advance so I'm not reaching for vending machine options at 3am.

03
Body

I hydrate before I caffeinate

16–24 oz of water before any coffee — every day. By the time we feel thirsty, we're already mildly dehydrated. Dehydration affects cognition, mood, kidney function, and energy. As a physician, I need to be sharp. Water first is non-negotiable.

04
Preventive

I actually go to my own doctor

Physicians are notoriously bad patients. I have a PCP. I go. I get my labs. I have a dentist. I stay current on my screenings. You cannot advocate for your patients' preventive care while skipping your own. It's also humbling — and important.

05
Mind

I take my days off seriously

Time off isn't a reward — it's a physiological reset. I protect post-call days and off days from work emails, clinical thinking, and content obligations when I can. Burnout is real, and recovery happens in the margins.

06
Body

I move my body even when I don't want to

I walk miles during a night shift, but that's not exercise — that's occupation. Intentional movement (even 20–30 minutes) improves mood, insulin sensitivity, cardiovascular health, and sleep quality. I don't wait to feel motivated. I go anyway.

07
Mind

I limit doom-scrolling before sleep

Blue light and stimulating content before sleep suppress melatonin and delay sleep onset. I try to put my phone down 30–60 minutes before sleeping. I'm not perfect at this — but I'm intentional about it, because I've seen what chronic sleep disruption does to the body.

08
Mind

I protect my relationships — especially family time

Medicine will take everything you give it and ask for more. I've learned to set boundaries that protect the people who matter most. Family time isn't what's left after work — it's something I schedule and defend. Healthy relationships are genuinely protective against burnout, depression, and even cardiovascular disease. Connection is medicine.

09
Systems

I keep a running list of my own medications and allergies

If something happens to me, I want every provider who touches me to have accurate information immediately. A simple note in my phone: current medications, allergies, medical history, emergency contacts. It takes 10 minutes and it could save your life.

10
Mind

I process the weight of this work

Medicine is heavy. I witness loss, difficult conversations, and moral complexity regularly. I talk to people I trust. I journal. I recognize when I'm carrying too much. Emotional processing isn't soft — it's essential for longevity in this field and in life.

Guide 02

Top 10 Lab Values
Everyone Should Know

Your lab results contain a story about your health — but most people never learn to read it. Here are the 10 most important values, what they mean, and why they matter.

"When my patients ask 'are my labs normal?' I want them to already understand what normal means. Knowledge is the first line of defense." — Dr. Ijay
Lab Test Normal Range Why It Matters
Metabolic
Hemoglobin A1c
HbA1c / Glycated Hemoglobin
< 5.7% Pre-diabetes: 5.7–6.4%
Diabetes: ≥ 6.5%
Reflects your average blood sugar over the past 3 months. One of the most important tests for catching diabetes early — before symptoms appear. Everyone over 35 should know this number.
Cardiac
LDL Cholesterol
Low-Density Lipoprotein
< 100 mg/dL Optimal for most adults.
Higher risk = lower target.
The "bad" cholesterol that builds up in artery walls and drives heart attacks and strokes. Your target depends on your overall cardiovascular risk — ask your doctor what your personal goal should be.
Kidney
eGFR
Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate
≥ 60 mL/min Declining trend matters
as much as the number.
Measures how well your kidneys are filtering waste. Chronic kidney disease is often silent until advanced. Catching a declining eGFR early allows you to slow or stop the damage — lifestyle and medication both help.
Blood
Hemoglobin
Hgb (part of CBC)
12–17.5 g/dL Range varies by sex
and age.
Carries oxygen to every cell in your body. Low hemoglobin = anemia, which causes fatigue, shortness of breath, and brain fog. Many people have no idea they're anemic until a routine lab reveals it.
Metabolic
Fasting Glucose
Blood Sugar (Fasting)
70–99 mg/dL Pre-diabetes: 100–125
Diabetes: ≥ 126
A snapshot of your blood sugar after fasting. Elevated fasting glucose is often the first measurable sign of insulin resistance — years before diabetes develops. Caught early, it's reversible with lifestyle changes.
Liver
ALT
Alanine Aminotransferase
7–56 U/L Elevated = possible
liver stress or damage.
A liver enzyme that rises when liver cells are inflamed or damaged. Elevated ALT can signal fatty liver disease, alcohol-related liver damage, medication effects, or viral hepatitis — often with zero symptoms.
Kidney
Creatinine
Serum Creatinine
0.6–1.2 mg/dL Higher in men and
those with more muscle mass.
A waste product cleared by the kidneys. When kidneys struggle, creatinine rises. This value is used to calculate your eGFR. Trending upward over time is a red flag even if the value stays "in range."
Critical
Sodium
Serum Sodium (Na⁺)
135–145 mEq/L Outside this range
can be dangerous.
Controls fluid balance in every cell of your body. Abnormal sodium — high or low — causes confusion, seizures, and can be life-threatening. It's often a clue that something bigger is going on, from dehydration to heart or kidney failure.
Cardiac
TSH
Thyroid Stimulating Hormone
0.4–4.0 mIU/L Interpretation depends
on clinical context.
Thyroid dysfunction is incredibly common — especially in women — and affects energy, weight, mood, heart rate, and fertility. Abnormal TSH is often dismissed until symptoms become impossible to ignore. Know your number.
Blood
Vitamin D
25-Hydroxyvitamin D
30–100 ng/mL Deficiency: < 20 ng/mL
Very common in the US.
Vitamin D deficiency is epidemic — affecting bone health, immune function, mood, and potentially cardiovascular risk. Most people are low and don't know it. A simple blood test and supplement can make a meaningful difference.

Guide 03 · Cheat Sheet

How to Read Your
Discharge Papers

You just spent time in the hospital. You're exhausted, overwhelmed, and handed a stack of papers on your way out the door. Here's what to actually look for — so you don't end up back in the ER within 30 days.

Why this matters: Roughly 1 in 5 Medicare patients is readmitted to the hospital within 30 days of discharge. Many of those readmissions are preventable. They happen because instructions weren't clear, medications weren't filled, or follow-up appointments weren't made. This guide helps you close those gaps.

Step 1 · Find These Documents First
After Visit Summary (AVS) Usually the first page. Contains your diagnosis, what happened, and the basics. Start here.
Discharge Instructions Condition-specific care instructions. Read every word. This is what your nurse reviewed with you before you left.
Medication Reconciliation List Every medication you are supposed to take going home — including new ones, stopped ones, and changed doses.
Follow-Up Appointment Sheet Who you need to see, when, and why. These appointments are not optional — they are part of your treatment.
Step 2 · Understand Your Diagnosis
What was my primary diagnosis? Look for "Discharge Diagnosis" or "Principal Diagnosis." This is what the team determined was wrong. Write it down in plain language you understand.
What tests or procedures were done? Look for a "Results" or "Procedures" section. Were there results still pending when you left? Those need follow-up.
Are there any pending results? Cultures, biopsies, and some labs may not be back by the time you leave. These MUST be followed up — confirm who is tracking them.
Step 3 · Review Every Medication
Which medications are NEW? Highlight anything you weren't taking before. Know the name, dose, what it's for, and for how long you should take it.
Which medications were STOPPED? Sometimes home medications are held during hospitalization and not restarted. Confirm with your doctor before resuming anything that's missing from the list.
Did any doses CHANGE? A medication you were on before might be at a different dose now. Do not assume your old prescription is still correct.
Fill prescriptions before you go home Do not wait until you're feeling worse. Many hospitals have on-site pharmacies or can coordinate with your pharmacy before discharge.
Step 4 · Lock In Your Follow-Up
Schedule your follow-up appointment within 7 days Studies show patients who see a doctor within 7 days of discharge are significantly less likely to be readmitted. Call the next day if you have to.
Know who to call with questions Your discharge papers should include a contact number. Save it in your phone before you leave the building.
Bring your discharge papers to every appointment The doctors you see after discharge may not have access to your hospital records yet. Be your own advocate — bring the paperwork.
🔴 Return to the ER Immediately If You Experience:
  • Chest pain, pressure, or tightness
  • Sudden shortness of breath or difficulty breathing
  • Signs of infection: fever over 101°F, increasing redness, warmth, or pus at any wound site
  • Severe abdominal pain, repeated vomiting, or inability to keep fluids down
  • New confusion, weakness, or difficulty speaking
  • Symptoms that are the same or worse than what brought you in
Quick Reference · Questions to Ask Before Leaving
"What is my diagnosis in plain English?" You deserve to understand what happened to your body.
"Are there any test results still pending?" And who will contact me when they come back?
"What symptoms should make me come back?" Get specific — don't leave without knowing your personal warning signs.
"Is there anything I should NOT do when I go home?" Activity restrictions, dietary limits, driving — ask explicitly.
"Who do I call if I have questions tonight?" Get the after-hours number before you walk out.
